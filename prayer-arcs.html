The Astronomy of Prayer
01
Fajr · Dawn
The Most Curved Arc
Fajr begins when the sun is 18° below the horizon — a moment called astronomical twilight. At this angle, the sky begins to faint from black to the deepest navy, barely perceptible to the eye.

Of all five prayers, Fajr produces the most dramatically curved arc. This is because the sun's angle of approach to the horizon changes the most with latitude — near the poles in summer, the sun never dips 18° below, so Fajr ceases to exist astronomically. The arc terminates abruptly where this occurs.

On the globe, Fajr appears as a deep blue wave sweeping westward each day, racing just ahead of the sun's rising.
"And eat and drink until the white thread of dawn becomes distinct to you from the black thread of night." — Quran 2:187
02
Dhuhr · Solar Noon
The Vertical Line
Dhuhr is the only prayer that produces a perfectly vertical arc — a straight line on the map. This is because Dhuhr begins at solar noon, the moment the sun reaches its highest point in the sky (the zenith). This moment is purely a function of longitude, not latitude.

Every mosque on the same meridian experiences solar noon at exactly the same instant. The gold vertical wave on the globe moves westward at exactly 15° of longitude per hour — the same rate as Earth's rotation.

Interestingly, solar noon almost never coincides with clock noon — the Equation of Time can shift it by up to ±16 minutes throughout the year.
The gold wave on AdhanLive is the most clocklike of all prayers — moving with the mathematical precision of Earth's rotation.
03
Asr · Afternoon
The Shadow Theorem
Asr is the most mathematically elegant prayer. Rather than a fixed angle, it is defined by a shadow ratio: Asr begins when an object's shadow equals its own length plus its shadow at solar noon. This is the Shafi, Maliki, and Hanbali position — the Hanafi madhab uses shadow = 2× the object.

This definition creates a curved arc that changes character dramatically with the seasons. In summer, when the sun is high, Asr comes relatively late in the afternoon. In winter, when the sun is low, Asr arrives much earlier — sometimes only an hour after Dhuhr.

On the map, Asr's arc is distinctly S-shaped, reflecting the changing solar declination.
The Prophet ﷺ said: "Whoever prays the two cool prayers (Fajr and Asr) will go to Paradise." — Bukhari
04
Maghrib · Sunset
The Precise Moment
Maghrib is the most universally agreed-upon prayer time — all four madhabs agree it begins at actual sunset, when the upper limb of the sun disappears below the horizon. Astronomically, this is when the sun is at −0.833° (accounting for atmospheric refraction and the sun's angular radius).

Unlike Fajr and Isha, Maghrib never disappears at high latitudes in the same way — the sun always sets (except at the poles). However, in summer near the Arctic Circle, Maghrib can occur very late, sometimes after 11 PM local time.

The red wave on AdhanLive is the most dramatic — it sweeps across the terminator line, precisely tracing where day becomes night on Earth.
Maghrib traces the sunset terminator — the same boundary that Apollo astronauts photographed from space.
05
Isha · Night
The Widest Wave
Isha begins when the red twilight (shafaq) has completely disappeared — when the sun is 17–18° below the horizon depending on the calculation method used. Like Fajr, it is defined by twilight angle, but on the opposite side of the night.

Isha is often described as the "widest" prayer in terms of simultaneous global coverage. Because it spans from late sunset to before midnight, it covers the broadest arc of longitude at any given moment — the purple wave on AdhanLive covers up to 40% of Earth's surface simultaneously.
There are at least 7 different Isha angle values used globally — from 15° to 18° — explaining why Isha times can differ by up to 30 minutes between apps for the same location.
The Equation of Time
Why Solar Noon Moves
All prayer times are anchored to solar noon — but solar noon itself is not fixed. Due to Earth's elliptical orbit and axial tilt, the sun runs up to 16 minutes fast or slow compared to a perfect clock throughout the year. This is called the Equation of Time.

Islamic astronomers understood this centuries before modern timekeeping. The seasonal breathing of the arcs you see in the visualizer above is partly due to this effect — the arc positions shift subtly each day even at the same latitude, a direct consequence of orbital mechanics.
The figure-8 path traced by the sun at the same clock time each day — called the Analemma — is a direct visualization of the Equation of Time. Muslim astronomers called it taqwīm al-shams.
The High Latitude Problem
Where Fajr
Disappears
Above roughly 48°N latitude, something remarkable happens in summer: the sun never dips far enough below the horizon for Fajr and Isha to occur astronomically. The sky never fully darkens. These prayers have no astronomical trigger.

This is not a modern problem — Islamic scholars have grappled with it for centuries, particularly as Islam spread to Northern Europe, Scandinavia, and Canada. The question "when do you pray when the sun doesn't set?" has produced several scholarly solutions:
Nearest Day Method Use prayer times from the last day when times were astronomically calculable — typically the nearest spring/autumn equinox period.
Makkah / Madinah Method Use the prayer times of Makkah or Madinah for all affected locations, as the spiritual center of Islam.
One-Seventh of Night Divide the night into sevenths and assign the first seventh to Isha and the last to Fajr — a purely mathematical solution endorsed by some scholars.
Angle-Based Estimation Use a fixed twilight angle even when the sun technically doesn't reach it, extrapolating from the nearest normal period.
